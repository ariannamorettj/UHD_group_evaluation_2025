Una Her Doc Group Evaluation
Decolonizing Ethnographic Collections in European Museums
Paris, 4-5 March 2025
Discover Our Project

Project Overview

European ethnographic museums experienced significant growth during the 19th century in a colonial context. Over the past 40 years, with the rise of post-colonial studies, the issue of decolonizing these problematic collections has become more relevant than ever.

Emmanuel Macron’s speech in Ouagadougou in 2017, advocating for a comprehensive restitution policy for artworks looted during the colonial period, sparked intense discussions on the practical implementation of such restitutions. The Sarr-Savoy report, first published in 2018, marked a turning point in collective awareness and highlighted the need to reassess the collections held in our ethnographic museums.

Using the examples of restituted artworks to Benin and Senegal, and the challenges posed by the principle of inalienability of French national collections—necessitating a specific law for selective restitutions—we will explore how various European museums have adapted and developed different strategies to address these sometimes still-taboo issues.

These include the reshaping of the permanent collections at the Grassi Museum in Leipzig and the AfricaMuseum in Brussels, and the highly controversial approach taken by the British Museum on the matter.

Topic Introduction

“A museum is a not-for-profit, permanent institution in the service of society that researches, collects, conserves, interprets and exhibits tangible and intangible heritage. Open to the public, accessible and inclusive, museums foster diversity and sustainability. They operate and communicate ethically, professionally and with the participation of communities, offering varied experiences for education, enjoyment, reflection and knowledge sharing.”
- ICOM, 2022

Nowadays, museums are central institutions for Western culture, and they are being increasingly criticized for their past in perpetuating colonial narratives and the ethical responsibilities they bear in addressing past injustices. A certain number of cultural artifacts that have been on display may have been acquired through imperial conquest, scientific expeditions, or unequal exchanges. Their display and conservation contribute to humanity due to their significant and shared historical value, uniting people beyond their political and religious differences.

"The absence of heritage can render memory silent and make it difficult to work on the history of young nations, which must confront the delicate challenge of building a political community and shaping a vision for the future."
- Felwine Sarr & Bénédicte Savoy [1]

During the 1950s, many nations gained independence, moved beyond the colonial era, and called for the return and restitution of their cultural heritage. A 1966 study conducted by the International Council of Museums (ICOM) highlighted key issues:

"The problem stems from a dual reality. On the one hand, museums in Europe and North America hold vast collections of artworks from the world, amassed through extensive missions over the past centuries that enabled significant acquisitions. On the other hand, many newly independent nations, striving to build museums that reflect their own cultural heritage as well as global influences, often have very limited collections in this latter category."
- [2]

The movement of the restitution of colonial era artifacts has gained monumental importance in recent decades by calling for the return and restitution of looted cultural heritage from the African and Asian communities. A very characteristic example is the 2018 Sarr-Savoy report commissioned by the French government, which advocated for the systematic return of colonial objects held in European museums.[3]

Major cultural institutions such as the British Museum, the Louvre, and Germany’s Humboldt Forum have been under scrutiny, as their collections contain significant cultural and historical artifacts acquired during colonial rule.[4]

The four most frequently debated arguments regarding this topic are:

  • The Legal Argument

    In most cases, the cultural objects in question were acquired through legal means, in accordance with the legal frameworks and geopolitical contexts of the time. However, if countries holding these objects were to return them, would this not imply an acknowledgment of their illicit acquisition? This concern often highlights the need for legislative changes. For example, French legislation maintains the inalienability of national collections, meaning that once an object enters a public museum, it cannot be legally sold, restituted, or transferred. Therefore, in such cases, restitution efforts are highly complex.[5]

  • The Museological Argument

    Some holding nations argue that the requesting countries lack the necessary infrastructure and resources to properly preserve the cultural artifacts they seek to reclaim. Would it not be better, then, to keep these objects in well-equipped museums where they are safeguarded?

  • The Universalist Argument

    Cultural artifacts from requesting nations are housed in globally recognized museums in industrialized countries, where they are accessible and appreciated by a vast audience.

  • The Technical Argument

    The return and restitution of cultural property are often facilitated through direct and discreet negotiations between museum professionals rather than through legal or political disputes.[5]

Despite these controversial arguments, a major part of the international community increasingly supports the restitution of cultural heritage. There has been a shift in attitude among former colonial and importing countries[6], signaling greater openness to addressing historical injustices.[7]

The launch of the “Appel en faveur du retour d’un patrimoine culturel irremplaçable à ceux qui l’ont créé” by the former general director of UNESCO, M. M’Bow (1978), placed this issue at the center of debates because it combines legal, diplomatic, and moral aspects.[8]

Several museums have adopted decolonization efforts, including provenance research, artistic interventions, and collaborative exhibitions with source communities. For example, the AfricaMuseum in Belgium has undergone major renovations to present a more critical perspective on its colonial history.[9]

Similarly, the Grassi Museum in Leipzig has taken steps to return the Benin bronzes and engage in dialogue with Nigerian institutions.[10] Meanwhile, the Te Fare Imanaha Museum in Tahiti has explored the concept of ‘ambassador objects’ as a cultural diplomacy policy, balancing demands for restitution with ongoing international collaborations.[11]

AfricaMuseum in Brussels

Context on the Nation-State

While Belgium is a relatively new State, gaining its independence only in 1830, it has played an active and bloody role in the colonization of the African continent, specifically in the Congo but later also with influences in Rwanda and Burundi. Belgium provides a unique case study of colonialism and imperialism, as the Congo was originally colonized to serve as the private property of King Leopold II between 1885 and 1908, thus excluding an official role for the Belgian State, but later became an official Belgian colony from 1908 and 1960, after increasing international diplomatic pressure due to violence used by colonial officers and the merciless economic extraction of rubber and ivory from the country.[12]

Appropriately remembering this colonial past comes with its own challenges, as an individual figure, King Leopold II, played a central role in the colonization of the Congo as its sole owner, but the role of the Belgian State should also not be downplayed. While King Leopold II passed away at the height of his unpopularity in 1909, the outbreak of the First World War led to the gravity of his crimes being forgotten. In fact, after the First World War, the Belgian government relied on the imagery of King Leopold II to strengthen its own colonial claims, thus leading to an increase in monuments dedicated to him in the Belgian public space.[13]

Public debate that has taken place in this European country raised by contestations around a monument, statue, exhibition or museum collections

In Belgium, protests against the continued colonial presence in the public space already emerged around 2004, more than a decade before the widely spread Rhodes Must Fall Movement in 2015. This public outrage, and subsequent vandalization of a few colonial monuments, was partly caused by the publication of the bestseller King Leopold’s Ghost, which brought Belgium’s colonial past to the attention of a broader audience, and the Belgian book The Assassination of Lumumba, which highlights the role of the Belgian government in the 1961 killing of the first prime minister of the Republic of Congo.[13]

After the VRT, the Flemish public broadcasting service, further exposed the atrocities authorized by King Leopold II in the Congo Free State in April 2004, three activists cut off the hand of one of the figures in a monument dedicated to King Leopold II in Ostend. Twenty years onwards, the hand is still missing – the activists are planning to literally take the hand to the grave with them unless the monument is removed to a museum which provides more context – and the figure was never restored [""].

In the 2010s, protests continued to grow against the continued presence of King Leopold II in the Belgian public space, including the defacing of his equestrian statue in Brussels with red paint in 2008, 2013, and 2015 [""].

Artists and activists also relied on temporary sculptures to make their point, such as the construction of an ice sculpture of King Leopold II to symbolize the need for his presence to melt away [""] or the reconstruction of a bust out of bird seed so it would slowly be pecked away by birds [""].

Since the Black Lives Matter movement in 2020, there has been a rapid increase in criticism regarding the colonial statues still standing in Belgium. While public authorities continue to repair damaged monuments, there has also been a gradually growing number of plaques being added since 2009 to provide more context. Moreover, since 2018, public squares and streets have also increasingly been renamed, and since 2020, more statues have been removed[13].

In 2020, the Belgian Federal Parliament also established the “Colonial Past Commission” [""].

Alternative heritage practices to make visible the trouble around these heritage monuments/objects (artistic intervention, walking tour)

The AfricaMuseum in Brussels finds its origins in the International Exposition of 1897. After the Exposition, King Leopold II requested the move of the ‘Colonial Section’ to the Africa Palace in Tervuren, including geographic samples, animals and ethnographic objects. In the surrounding park, an African village was recreated housing several Congolese individuals, seven of whom died while living there.

For King Leopold II, the museum was a propaganda tool to garner support for his imperial project, and the Africa Palace became a permanent museum of the Congo in 1898, including an institute for scientific research. In 1908, after the Congo Free State became the Belgian Congo, the museum was renamed as the Museum of Belgian Congo until it became the Royal Museum of the Belgian Congo in 1952.

After the Congo declared its independence in 1960, the museum was rechristened again to the Royal Museum of Central Africa [""].

Between 2015 and 2018, the museum was closed for renovations to update the exhibition and infrastructure to present decolonized perspectives on Africa in a setting which had historically been constructed as colonial propaganda [""].

Since 2018, the museum communicates, and is more commonly known, under the name AfricaMuseum, even though its official name is still the Royal Museum for Central Africa.

As the building housing the collection is listed as a protected immovable heritage site, the museum struggled to sufficiently alter the space during the renovations to create a decolonized space. For instance, the grand rotunda contains multiple colonial statues, but when the AfricaMuseum was considering their removal, the Flanders Heritage Agency argued that this would damage an integral part of the listed building.

In response, the AfricaMuseum launched an international competition for an art piece which would counterbalance these colonial statues. The work of Aimé Mpane, a Congolese artist, was selected, but soon after the installation of his work, visitors continued to indicate the decolonization of the space was insufficient. Moreover, the UN Human Rights Council called on the museum to remove all colonial propaganda. Following these reactions, Aimé Mpane constructed a second sculpture in the space and worked with Jean-Pierre Müller, a Belgian artist, to hang sixteen semi-transparent veils in front of the existing statues with prints of contemporary images, causing a juxtaposition and new reading of the colonial and immovable heritage [""].

Current Stance on Colonization

Today, the AfricaMuseum describes its position on colonization as follows:

“Colonialism is a form of government based on military occupation, authoritarian and racist administration, and exploitation. The museum therefore explicitly distances itself from it. It takes responsibility for the impact that its previous propaganda for colonialism has had on the multicultural society of today, and for the message of Western moral and intellectual superiority it has conveyed in the past. The museum intends to be a forum for opinions and views on the historical, current and future relationship with Central Africa” [""].

Restitution and Legal Ownership

The AfricaMuseum itself cannot make any decisions affecting the ownership of its collections, since the Belgian Federal State remains their legal owner. Decisions on restitution must thus be made by the Federal State Secretary for Science Policy in accordance with Belgian legislation and with parliamentary approval.

A bill was adopted by the Federal Parliament in July 2022 specifying the need for a treaty between the Belgian State and the State of origin before the restitution of property can be made possible. Such a treaty is currently being drafted between Belgium and the Congo, and would require the establishment of a joint Congolese-Belgian commission to assess the provenance of the objects concerned [""].

Provenance Research and Collections

Since September 2022, the AfricaMuseum has been working on a research project regarding the provenance of the museum’s ethnographic collections together with the Institut des Musées Nationaux du Congo. Moreover, in 2021, the AfricaMuseum launched a ‘provenance trail’ with a smartphone-guided tour to bring its provenance research results to visitors of the collection [""].

Human Remains and Ethical Concerns

In its collection, the AfricaMuseum also houses two mummified persons since 1919. Previously, the museum also still held other skulls and human remains, although most were moved in 1964 to the Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences, where they can still be found today, after the museum’s department of physical anthropology was closed.

Moreover, the museum also has several ethnographic objects partly made from human remains, such as a horn with a piece of jawbone. The AfricaMuseum collaborates with six other institutions to evaluate the background of these remains as part of the HOME project (Human Remains Origin(s) Multidisciplinary Evaluation) [""].

The Grassi Museum of Leipzig

A critical approach of ethnographic collection

Currently holding about 350,000 objects, 200,000 images, and 350,000 library elements, the three ethnological museums in Leipzig, Dresden, and Herrnhut form the second-largest collection of its kind in Germany. These museums, part of the Saxon State Art Collections (Staatliche Kunstsammlungen Dresden), have been actively examining their colonial past since 2010. Decolonization aims to dismantle colonial structures of discrimination, both tangible and embedded in thought processes. It is an active process open to everyone.

In February 2021, the German Museum Association (Museumsbund) published a guide for German museums [""]. This guide serves as a comprehensive resource for museum professionals, helping them adopt ethical and informed practices in managing colonial collections while fostering respectful cooperation with source communities. The main areas addressed include:

  • Collection and Conservation Practices: The guide emphasizes the importance of transparency in collection and conservation processes. It encourages museums to meticulously document the origin of objects and ensure their preservation respects the cultural sensitivities of the communities concerned.
  • Provenance Research: Understanding the history and context of object acquisition is essential. The guide recommends close collaboration with source societies to enrich knowledge about collections and ensure faithful interpretations of their cultural significance.
  • Exhibition and Cultural Mediation: Museums are encouraged to present objects with consideration for the perspectives of source communities. This involves inclusive narratives reflecting the cultural and historical meanings of objects, avoiding stereotypes or inappropriate representations.
  • Restitution: The guide addresses restitution issues by providing practical examples of successful collaborations between museums and source communities. It highlights the importance of open, respectful dialogue to achieve mutually satisfactory solutions for object return or alternative forms of compensation.
  • Public Policies and Guidelines: The document compiles international agreements, guidelines on indigenous community involvement, state-funded programs, and museum-specific recommendations, providing a legal and ethical framework for institutions.
  • Databases and Additional Resources: The guide lists online databases and recommended readings to deepen understanding of handling collections from colonial contexts.

The Grassi Museum für Völkerkunde in Leipzig has notably undertaken initiatives to confront its colonial past and promote transparency in the process of artifact restitution. Among the notable actions taken by the Grassi Museum for the decolonization of its collections, the following initiatives stand out:

  • Restitution of the Benin Bronzes

    The museum acknowledges the historical and moral necessity of returning the Benin Bronzes, objects acquired during the colonial era. However, the final restitution decision lies with the German government. The museum plays an advisory role and actively participates in restitution discussions. For the Grassi Museum, restitution is not the end but the beginning of a new collaboration. They explore opportunities for mutual learning with Nigerian colleagues, co-develop restoration projects, and raise public awareness in Leipzig about the issue [""].

  • Online Decolonization Platform

    The museum has launched a platform dedicated to decolonization, restitution, and repatriation. This initiative aims to enhance transparency and accessibility, allowing stakeholders to learn about past and ongoing projects. The platform also facilitates specific collection-related requests, encouraging collaborative provenance research and coordinated repatriation processes. The museum considers restitution and repatriation as central missions and has already returned human remains to Hawaii and Australia, with more projects in progress [""].

  • Reevaluating the Term «Völkerkunde»

    The term 'Völkerkunde' (ethnology) reflects a colonial worldview. The museum acknowledges the importance of critically assessing this history as part of its repositioning efforts. A name influences the museum’s perception, visitor reception, and overall identity. Engaged in a renewal process, the museum collaborates with various stakeholders to redefine its identity, aiming to become a 'network museum' that reflects on its history and contributes to contemporary and future issues [""].

  • Presentation of Colonial Objects

    Exhibiting objects from the colonial era requires dialogue with concerned communities. Collaborative projects can develop approaches to present these objects, tell their stories, and encourage discourse while respecting the sensitivities of source communities.

In summary, the Grassi Museum für Völkerkunde in Leipzig is actively committed to decolonization, transparency, and collaboration with source communities to confront its colonial past and envision an inclusive and respectful future.

In a press release on March 5, 2021, Léontine Meijer-van Mensch, former director of the Saxon State Ethnographic Collections, stated:

« The ethnological discourse is ongoing; it is alive. It is a process that still requires in-depth research. We need to reflect and talk about our ambivalent past. For example, does a project end when we return the remains of a community member, or is it the beginning of long-term engagement involving not only the past but also the present and the future ? »
[""]

The Te Fare Iamanaha Museum of Tahiti, and the concept of “ambassador object”

A diplomatic approach of restitution?

The Fare Iamanaha – Museum of Tahiti and the Islands (MTI) was renamed Fare Iamanaha upon its reopening in March 2023. This name change, emphasizing the Tahitian language, reflects a desire to assert a renewed cultural identity[14].

Recent developments at the MTI, including the reconstruction of the permanent exhibition hall ["] and projects to repatriate Polynesian objects previously held in institutions in Paris, London, and Cambridge, illustrate this new dynamic of cultural reappropriation in Polynesia. These initiatives have strengthened ties between the MTI and museums such as the Musée du quai Branly – Jacques Chirac, the British Museum, and the Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology at the University of Cambridge.

This connection is notably embodied by the concept of the “ambassador object” — Polynesian artifacts that, when exhibited abroad, serve as cultural representatives, fostering relationships between museums and promoting a form of cultural sovereignty that transcends physical presence. This approach contrasts with a sovereignty focused solely on the physical restitution of artifacts.

The concept of “ambassador objects” is based on the idea that certain heritage objects, even when not physically present in their place of origin, can still play an active role in preserving and promoting a people’s cultural identity. For instance, artifacts of the MTI were displayed as ambassador objects in major western museums. Rather than viewing it simply as an artifact removed from its original context, this concept is seen as an opportunity to showcase Polynesian culture on the global stage. The object thus becomes an “ambassador”: it tells the story of the people who created it, draws attention to their heritage, and raises worldwide awareness of Polynesian traditions [11].

This approach offers very concrete benefits:

  • Global cultural visibility: Objects exhibited abroad carry the voice of their cultures, sparking curiosity and understanding among visitors.
  • Partnerships and exchanges: Collaborations between local and international museums facilitate knowledge exchange, joint research, and sometimes artifact loans for temporary exhibitions.
  • Cultural sovereignty without immediate physical restitution: Rather than focusing solely on the return of objects, this approach acknowledges that culture can thrive even at a distance while advocating for forms of shared or co-managed collections.

This does not mean that restitution is unimportant. But this vision nuances the debate: cultural sovereignty is not limited to physically possessing objects. It can also be built by affirming communities’ ability to tell their own stories, even when their artifacts are abroad, while ensuring that these objects are treated with respect and properly contextualized in Western museums.

This is a form of cultural diplomacy, where artifacts become bridges between cultures rather than mere objects of possession. It also shows that restoring symbolic ties to objects can sometimes be just as powerful as material restitution.

The collaborative exhibition “Maro ‘ura. A Polynesian Treasure” (2021–2022) at the Musée du quai Branly – Jacques Chirac is an example of this policy. The Maro’ura (a sacred royal belt) was exhibited as an ambassador object. Rather than being limited to a permanent return, the temporary exhibition of the Maro helped to illustrate the cooperation between institutions to enhance Polynesian heritage ["].

In conclusion, the MTI, as a cultural institution, plays a central role in redefining Polynesian cultural sovereignty. By encouraging the circulation of collections and establishing international partnerships, the museum actively contributes to the promotion and recognition of Polynesian heritage on the global stage.

Conclusions

These three case studies show how museums interpret the colonial past and are working towards their reconciliation.

  1. The AfricaMuseum is being renovated to tie Belgium's colonial past into the exhibition. The museum has decolonized aspects of its space—including installation art to counterbalance colonial statues, but the process continues. The museum has been decolonizing through discussions of returning cultural objects, showing that decolonization is a journey.

  2. The Grassi Museum of Leipzig is transparent through its engagement with source communities for the return of objects. It has continued to return items, such as the Benin Bronzes, and established an online platform to share decolonization information. Last but not least, the museum further reexamines its story and identity, while emphasizing dialogue and reflection as central to meaningful change.

  3. The Te Fare Iamanaha Museum of Tahiti and the concept of the "ambassador object" is discussed. It is more of an instrument to showcase the Polynesian culture to the world while creating strong cultural linkages and sovereignty that deal with such objects without having to return them. Also in that case, highlighting another aspect of cultural diplomacy and collaborations between museums even though in rare cases, there could be a requirement for physical restitution.

All three cases demonstrate that decolonization at museums goes beyond object-repatriation; it paves the way toward a much more inclusive, respectful, and still dialogue regarding history. Each museum is carving out its own way to respect the cultures and communities affected by colonialism, which indeed becomes important for the future of cultural institutions and generations.

Something about four stunning researchers

Madeleine Daste

PhD Student - Université Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne; University of Bologna

With a background in art and archaeology from my Bachelor’s and first Master’s in History of Art, I specialized in cultural heritage preservation, particularly in conflict contexts. During my second Master’s in conservation-restoration, I developed a restoration protocol for ceramics damaged in war. My thesis examines bomb-induced damage on ceramics from the National Ceramic Museum in Sèvres and the MIC in Faenza, analyzing the effects of blast, shock, and fire on different materials. This research aims to improve restoration protocols, aiding professionals in sorting and restoring post-conflict debris efficiently.

Anna Demetriou

PhD Student - Université Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne; University of Bologna

Fascinated by the restitution and protection of cultural heritage, I am a PhD student in Cultural Heritage at the University Paris 1. I studied History of Art and Archaeology at the University of Western Brittany and earned a master’s in the History of Museums and Cultural Studies at Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne. My research has focused on the restitution of Cypriot cultural property post-1974 and the effectiveness of UNESCO’s “Return and Restitution” Committee, analyzing cases like the Boğazköy Sphinx and the Parthenon Sculptures. To gain professional experience, I have interned at museums in Cyprus and worked at the Permanent Delegation of Cyprus to UNESCO (2020–2023).

Emma Dhondt

PhD Student - KU Leuven; University of Edinburgh

I am a PhD Researcher in International Law at KU Leuven, specializing in international cultural heritage law. My research focuses on the legal protection of cultural and natural heritage, both within and beyond the UNESCO World Heritage framework, with a focus on sub-national actors like Flanders and Scotland. I hold a Master in Laws and a Master in History from KU Leuven, specializing in public, international, and European law and cultural heritage. My previous work includes studies on World Heritage nominations in Eastern Europe and the portrayal of women in contested colonial monuments. I have also worked as a teaching assistant, coaching teams for international law competitions.

Arianna Moretti

PhD Student - University of Bologna; University of Helsinki

I hold a Bachelor’s in Anthropology, Religions, and Oriental Civilizations (2019) and a Master’s in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge (2022) from the University of Bologna, where I was also a Research Fellow (2022–2023). Currently, I am pursuing a Ph.D. in Cultural Heritage in the Digital Ecosystem while working as a teaching tutor for Basic Informatics and as a software developer at OpenCitations. My research focuses on automating metadata crosswalks for cultural heritage data using RDF and Semantic Web Technologies. I have developed Python plugins for OpenCitations to support data conversion and contribute to transforming tabular data into RDF for the CHANGES project.
