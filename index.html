Decolonizing Ethnographic Collections in European Museums
Paris, 4-5 March 2025
Discover Our Project

Project Overview

European ethnographic museums experienced significant growth during the 19th century in a colonial context. Over the past 40 years, with the rise of post-colonial studies, the issue of decolonizing these problematic collections has become more relevant than ever.

Emmanuel Macron’s speech in Ouagadougou in 2017, advocating for a comprehensive restitution policy for artworks looted during the colonial period, sparked intense discussions on the practical implementation of such restitutions. The Sarr-Savoy report, first published in 2018, marked a turning point in collective awareness and highlighted the need to reassess the collections held in our ethnographic museums.

Using the examples of restituted artworks to Benin and Senegal, and the challenges posed by the principle of inalienability of French national collections—necessitating a specific law for selective restitutions—we will explore how various European museums have adapted and developed different strategies to address these sometimes still-taboo issues.

These include the reshaping of the permanent collections at the Grassi Museum in Leipzig and the AfricaMuseum in Brussels, and the highly controversial approach taken by the British Museum on the matter.

Topic Introduction

“A museum is a not-for-profit, permanent institution in the service of society that researches, collects, conserves, interprets and exhibits tangible and intangible heritage. Open to the public, accessible and inclusive, museums foster diversity and sustainability. They operate and communicate ethically, professionally and with the participation of communities, offering varied experiences for education, enjoyment, reflection and knowledge sharing.”
- ICOM, 2022

Nowadays, museums are central institutions for Western culture, and they are being increasingly criticized for their past in perpetuating colonial narratives and the ethical responsibilities they bear in addressing past injustices. A certain number of cultural artifacts that have been on display may have been acquired through imperial conquest, scientific expeditions, or unequal exchanges. Their display and conservation contribute to humanity due to their significant and shared historical value, uniting people beyond their political and religious differences.

"The absence of heritage can render memory silent and make it difficult to work on the history of young nations, which must confront the delicate challenge of building a political community and shaping a vision for the future."
- Felwine Sarr & Bénédicte Savoy [1]

During the 1950s, many nations gained independence, moved beyond the colonial era, and called for the return and restitution of their cultural heritage. A 1966 study conducted by the International Council of Museums (ICOM) highlighted key issues:

"The problem stems from a dual reality. On the one hand, museums in Europe and North America hold vast collections of artworks from the world, amassed through extensive missions over the past centuries that enabled significant acquisitions. On the other hand, many newly independent nations, striving to build museums that reflect their own cultural heritage as well as global influences, often have very limited collections in this latter category."
- [2]

The movement of the restitution of colonial era artifacts has gained monumental importance in recent decades by calling for the return and restitution of looted cultural heritage from the African and Asian communities. A very characteristic example is the 2018 Sarr-Savoy report commissioned by the French government, which advocated for the systematic return of colonial objects held in European museums.[3]

Major cultural institutions such as the British Museum, the Louvre, and Germany’s Humboldt Forum have been under scrutiny, as their collections contain significant cultural and historical artifacts acquired during colonial rule.[4]

The four most frequently debated arguments regarding this topic are:

  • The Legal Argument

    In most cases, the cultural objects in question were acquired through legal means, in accordance with the legal frameworks and geopolitical contexts of the time. However, if countries holding these objects were to return them, would this not imply an acknowledgment of their illicit acquisition? This concern often highlights the need for legislative changes. For example, French legislation maintains the inalienability of national collections, meaning that once an object enters a public museum, it cannot be legally sold, restituted, or transferred. Therefore, in such cases, restitution efforts are highly complex.[5]

  • The Museological Argument

    Some holding nations argue that the requesting countries lack the necessary infrastructure and resources to properly preserve the cultural artifacts they seek to reclaim. Would it not be better, then, to keep these objects in well-equipped museums where they are safeguarded?

  • The Universalist Argument

    Cultural artifacts from requesting nations are housed in globally recognized museums in industrialized countries, where they are accessible and appreciated by a vast audience.

  • The Technical Argument

    The return and restitution of cultural property are often facilitated through direct and discreet negotiations between museum professionals rather than through legal or political disputes.[5]

Despite these controversial arguments, a major part of the international community increasingly supports the restitution of cultural heritage. There has been a shift in attitude among former colonial and importing countries[6], signaling greater openness to addressing historical injustices.[7]

The launch of the “Appel en faveur du retour d’un patrimoine culturel irremplaçable à ceux qui l’ont créé” by the former general director of UNESCO, M. M’Bow (1978), placed this issue at the center of debates because it combines legal, diplomatic, and moral aspects.[8]

Several museums have adopted decolonization efforts, including provenance research, artistic interventions, and collaborative exhibitions with source communities. For example, the AfricaMuseum in Belgium has undergone major renovations to present a more critical perspective on its colonial history.[9]

Similarly, the Grassi Museum in Leipzig has taken steps to return the Benin bronzes and engage in dialogue with Nigerian institutions.[10] Meanwhile, the Te Fare Imanaha Museum in Tahiti has explored the concept of ‘ambassador objects’ as a cultural diplomacy policy, balancing demands for restitution with ongoing international collaborations.[11]

Team Members

Something about four stunning researchers

Madeleine Daste

PhD Student - Université Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne; University of Bologna

With a background in art and archaeology from my Bachelor’s and first Master’s in History of Art, I specialized in cultural heritage preservation, particularly in conflict contexts. During my second Master’s in conservation-restoration, I developed a restoration protocol for ceramics damaged in war. My thesis examines bomb-induced damage on ceramics from the National Ceramic Museum in Sèvres and the MIC in Faenza, analyzing the effects of blast, shock, and fire on different materials. This research aims to improve restoration protocols, aiding professionals in sorting and restoring post-conflict debris efficiently.

Anna Demetriou

PhD Student - Université Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne; University of Bologna

Fascinated by the restitution and protection of cultural heritage, I am a PhD student in Cultural Heritage at the University Paris 1. I studied History of Art and Archaeology at the University of Western Brittany and earned a master’s in the History of Museums and Cultural Studies at Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne. My research has focused on the restitution of Cypriot cultural property post-1974 and the effectiveness of UNESCO’s “Return and Restitution” Committee, analyzing cases like the Boğazköy Sphinx and the Parthenon Sculptures. To gain professional experience, I have interned at museums in Cyprus and worked at the Permanent Delegation of Cyprus to UNESCO (2020–2023).

Emma Dhondt

PhD Student - KU Leuven; University of Edinburgh

I am a PhD Researcher in International Law at KU Leuven, specializing in international cultural heritage law. My research focuses on the legal protection of cultural and natural heritage, both within and beyond the UNESCO World Heritage framework, with a focus on sub-national actors like Flanders and Scotland. I hold a Master in Laws and a Master in History from KU Leuven, specializing in public, international, and European law and cultural heritage. My previous work includes studies on World Heritage nominations in Eastern Europe and the portrayal of women in contested colonial monuments. I have also worked as a teaching assistant, coaching teams for international law competitions.

Arianna Moretti

PhD Student - University of Bologna; University of Helsinki

I hold a Bachelor’s in Anthropology, Religions, and Oriental Civilizations (2019) and a Master’s in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge (2022) from the University of Bologna, where I was also a Research Fellow (2022–2023). Currently, I am pursuing a Ph.D. in Cultural Heritage in the Digital Ecosystem while working as a teaching tutor for Basic Informatics and as a software developer at OpenCitations. My research focuses on automating metadata crosswalks for cultural heritage data using RDF and Semantic Web Technologies. I have developed Python plugins for OpenCitations to support data conversion and contribute to transforming tabular data into RDF for the CHANGES project.
